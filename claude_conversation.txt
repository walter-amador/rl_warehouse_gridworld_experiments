Why exploration bonus is more robust across layouts.

Epsilon-greedy's exploration is random — it relies on chance to stumble into the right states during the exploration phase. On a layout where the pickup station is behind a cluster of shelves, random exploration might not reach it enough times before epsilon decays. The policy locks in before it's ready.

The exploration bonus is targeted — it actively seeks out the least-visited state-action pairs. No matter how the layout changes, the agent will deliberately push toward unexplored corners of the state space. It doesn't rely on luck to find the pickup station; it hunts for it. That's why the gap grows on harder layouts — harder layouts punish random exploration more.

Your instinct about trusting exploration bonus on unseen layouts is the right engineering judgment. In a real warehouse, layouts change constantly. You want an algorithm whose exploration strategy adapts to the structure of the problem, not one that depends on a well-chosen epsilon schedule that was tuned for a specific layout.
